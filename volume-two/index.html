Trains go over our heads. Our windowless arch sits underneath the lines. Twisting iron pins. Shaking stone and ballast. Imagine no driver, screaming machinery, unstoppable. We hit our own harmonic lines. The grinding squall of train-to-track. Strings on frets. Our underground skyline is made of guitars, drums, pianos, violins. The ghost in our strings wants out.

Suppressed in the Soviet Union for twenty-six years, Mikhail Bulgakov’s masterpiece is an ironic parable on power and its corruption, on good and evil, and on human frailty and the strength of love.

Or, perhaps, these were not the words at all. Perhaps there were others to the same tune, all of them highly scandalous. But this isn’t really the point. The point is that a veritable babel broke loose in the Variety Theatre after the final scene. Militiamen ran towards Sempleyarov’s loge, the curious clambered up the barriers, there were infernal bursts of roaring laughter and wild shouts, drowned out by the golden jingling of the cymbals in the band. And the stage was suddenly empty. That swindler Fagot, along with the brazen tom, Behemoth, dissolved into thin air, vanished as utterly as the magician had vanished earlier, together with his faded armchair.
